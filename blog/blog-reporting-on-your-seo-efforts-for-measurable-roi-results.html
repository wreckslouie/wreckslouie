Reporting on your SEO efforts for measurable ROI results

A huge number of SEO practitioners claim that in order to generate a good SEO result, a combination of black magic, witchcraft and voodoo, and maybe a hell of a lot of experience, is needed. As it happens, generating a magic report is a small problem. The results are far from readable and tends to mysteriously disappear from time to time. SEO is a slow developing process, while at the same time, search engines are constantly evolving their algorithms, making their reports clearer.

What exactly does an SEO report entail? What are the consideration you have to take note off when you're crafting a report? Remember, a comprehensive report, with the right data analytics will only help both the marketing and sales teams better focus their efforts when reaching their goals.

Questions that need answering when writing an SEO report:

  1. Are your marketing efforts allowing you to reach organic search goals? Your goals should be based on an in-depth buyer persona and keyword research. Research which keywords work on your target buyer personas and what keywords will be most effective in converting this potential lead.
  2. Have a checklist. Keep track of which SEO tasks have been completed during this time frame.
  3. Did the SEO efforts help you obtain your company's goals? (eg. Obtain new contacts, leads, customers, revenue)
  4. Are there any new opportunities that are available to help optimize organic searches?
  5. Are there any new threats from your competitors that you need to take note off?
  6. Monitor the status of your website's hit rate. Are there improvements you can make to your website?â€¨

The key to a perfect SEO report is to determine your goals clearly. A report, that is of any value, must always remind you of the overall reason for the SEO investment in the first place and its purpose. To effectively show your progress, benchmarking certain metrics and analytics is key.

In academic terms, you need to set SMART goals. SMART = Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant and Timely. An example of a good SMART goal is:

Increasing organic search traffic by 15%, from last quarter's search of 1,000, for specific keywords (Family friendly holiday destinations this school holiday) over the following 2 months.

The goal is specific, they want to increase organic search. It is measurable, an increase of 15% from the original 1,000 searches. It is attainable as it is not an outrageous demand. The keywords are relevant to the industry (Travel agency) and it has a timeline in which to measure the results, which is over the next two months.â€¨

SEO reporting is a critical component of the digital marketing report. In order to maximise the report for both the marketing and sales teams, it has to be both detailed and reliable. With a SEO report like that, you and your company can measure the impact of any given campaign and translate that into viable ROI.
